# Maai — Evidence base

Primary sources for the problem Maai addresses: women's cardiac symptoms
are disproportionately dismissed and misdiagnosed. All statistics below
trace to primary sources — cite these, not social media reposts.

## Primary source

**British Heart Foundation (2019) — "Bias and Biology: How the gender gap
in heart disease is costing women's lives"**
- News release: https://www.bhf.org.uk/what-we-do/news-from-the-bhf/news-archive/2019/september/heart-attack-gender-gap-is-costing-womens-lives
- Briefing summary (PDF): https://www.bhf.org.uk/-/media/files/heart-matters/bias-and-biology-summary.pdf

## Key figures (all from the BHF briefing above)

- **Coronary heart disease kills twice as many women as breast cancer in
  the UK each year.** (Opener — reframes the assumed threat.)
- **8,243 preventable deaths:** BHF-funded research suggests the deaths of
  at least 8,243 women in England and Wales over a ten-year period could
  have been prevented through equitable cardiac treatment. (The injustice
  stat — the gap is care, not biology.)
- **50% more likely to be misdiagnosed:** women who suffer a heart attack
  are 50% more likely than men to receive a wrong initial diagnosis
  (study led by Prof. Chris Gale). (The mechanism stat — the specific
  problem Maai speaks to: being misheard.)
- **Women delay seeking help** longer than men, in part because symptoms
  aren't recognised as cardiac. (Why the "symptoms now → 999" line is
  non-negotiable: Maai must never add to that delay.)

## Supporting academic source

- 2025 peer-reviewed review (PMC): women are less likely to receive
  diagnostic imaging and intervention even with comparable clinical
  indicators; atypical presentations contribute to underdiagnosis.
  (Non-charity corroboration if a technical judge wants more than one org.)

## Register note

Maai maps a woman's own words to clinical vocabulary. It never diagnoses,
assesses, or concludes — interpretation rests with the clinician. These
sources justify the problem; they are never used to interpret an
individual's symptoms.
